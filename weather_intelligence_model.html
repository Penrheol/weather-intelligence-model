Summary Insights
About This Tool

This interactive model operationalises the prescriptive analytics framework described in Rogers (2026), applying Bayesian Decision Theory to quantify the economic and social value generated by weather intelligence across weather-sensitive sectors.

Theoretical Basis

Prescriptive models go beyond describing what happened (descriptive) or predicting what will happen (predictive) — they identify the best course of action under uncertainty. In weather-sensitive sectors, decision-makers choose actions that maximise expected profits or minimise expected costs, using meteorological information to reduce uncertainty (Frei et al. 2014; Wissuchek & Zschech 2025).

The value of weather forecasts does not come from the data itself — it emerges from improved decisions by stakeholders who apply weather intelligence (WMO 2015d; Rogers et al. 2026). Benefit-cost analysis, while a necessary tool for justifying NMHS investment, is not a sufficient measure of this value — it is structurally bilateral, misses cross-sector cascade effects, and cannot capture social, intellectual, or natural capital. A quantitative systematic review of 175 peer-reviewed studies confirms that published benefit estimates are highly variable and concentrated in high-income countries, identifying a persistent gap between academic evidence and the calculations used in policy decisions (Farkas et al. 2025).

Value Dimensions Modelled

For each sector, the tool estimates value across four dimensions identified in Frei et al. (2014):

  • ↑ Business profitability — increased economic value from optimal decision-making
  • ↓ Government spending — reduced resource use in providing public services
  • ↓ Damage avoidance — infrastructure, equipment, health, and life
  • ↓ Productivity losses — minimising disruption to economic activity

Living Lab Context

These models are starting points for a co-production approach within a Living Lab framework (ENOLL 2025; Lorenzo et al. 2021), involving citizens, academia, the private sector, and government in refining decision support tools. The Theory of Change connecting inputs to outcomes is described in Rogers (2026) Figure 7.

How to Use This Tool

Navigate to each sector tab and adjust the input parameters to match your national or regional context. The tool calculates estimated annual value from weather intelligence for each dimension. Use the Compare tab to see relative sector contributions. Export buttons generate plain-text summaries for reporting.
